## CUSTOMER INTERVIEW EXECUTION GUIDE

**Your four core questions are solid.[2][3]**

Here's how to structure them using The Mom Test approach:[2][3]

### INTERVIEW STRUCTURE (15-20 minutes)[2]

**Opening (2 minutes):**
- "Thanks for taking time. I want to understand how you're using [product] and what problems it solves for you."
- Build rapport, explain you're taking notes

**Question 1: Purchase Motivation (3 minutes)[3]**
- "Why did you buy this product?"
- Follow-up: "Walk me through the last time you encountered [the problem this solves]"
- Follow-up: "How were you solving this problem before?"
- Listen for: Specific situation, not abstract benefit

**Question 2: Problem Validation (3 minutes)[3]**
- "What specific problem does this solve for you?"
- Follow-up: "What does this problem cost you (time, money, stress)?"
- Follow-up: "If this problem was solved, what would that mean for you?"
- Listen for: Concrete impact, not hypothetical benefit

**Question 3: Recommendation Likelihood (3 minutes)[6]**
- "What would make you recommend this to a friend?"
- Follow-up: "What's the one feature that made you buy?"
- Follow-up: "Who else do you know with this problem?"
- Listen for: Perceived value, not feature list

**Question 4: Pain Points (3 minutes)[2][3]**
- "What's your biggest frustration with templates/workflows in general?"
- Follow-up: "What don't you love about your current solution?"
- Follow-up: "What would need to change for this to be 10x easier?"
- Listen for: Specific friction, not vague complaints

**Closing (1 minute):**
- "Is there anything else I should know?"
- Ask for referral to another customer
- Thank them

### DOCUMENTATION STRATEGY[5]

**For each interview, capture:**

✅ **Customer metadata:**
- Name, company, date, duration
- Product purchased ($89 template? higher tier?)
- How long they've been a customer

✅ **Direct quotes** (verbatim, in their own words)[5]
- Capture exact phrasing when they describe problems
- Note emotional tone (frustrated, excited, resigned)

✅ **Specific examples**[5]
- "Tell me about the last time..." responses
- Concrete use cases, not abstract benefits

✅ **Follow-up questions that emerged**[5]
- What surprised you?
- What did you probe deeper on?

---

### ANALYSIS PROCESS[2][3]

**After all 15 interviews:**

**Step 1: Extract key quotes**
- Pull verbatim quotes from each interview
- Organize by question (motivation, problem, recommendation, frustration)

**Step 2: Identify patterns**[3]
- Which pain points appear in 3+ interviews? (signal, not noise)
- Which problems are mentioned most frequently?
- Which solutions are customers currently using?
- Which features drove the purchase decision?

**Step 3: Document themes**[2][3]
- "5 customers mentioned [specific pain point]"
- "3 customers mentioned [specific feature] as the deciding factor"
- "All 15 customers mentioned [common frustration]"

**Step 4: Highlight surprises**[2][3]
- What did you expect to hear but didn't?
- What did you hear that you didn't expect?
- Where does reality differ from your assumptions?

---

### COMMON PITFALLS TO AVOID[1][4]

❌ **Don't ask hypothetical questions**
- "Would you use X?" → Ask "How are you currently solving this?"[1]
- "Would you pay $X?" → Ask "What did you pay for your current solution?"[1]

❌ **Don't lead the witness**
- "This feature is great, right?" → Ask "What do you think of this feature?"[2]

❌ **Don't talk too much**
- You should listen 80% of the time[2]
- Let silence sit—they'll fill it with real thoughts[2]

❌ **Don't assume they're using it the way you think**
- "How do you use [feature]?" → Listen for actual usage, not intended usage[1]
